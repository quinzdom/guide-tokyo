Step One

Getting Here

From Narita Airport international arrivals to your hotel. It sounds complicated. It is slightly complicated. But the trains run on time — embarrassingly on time — so just follow the signs and you'll be fine.

Recommended

Narita Express (N'EX)

~90 min total  ·  ~¥3,350  ·  1 transfer

The easy option. Reserved seats, luggage racks, direct to Shinjuku. Worth the extra cost after a long flight.

Budget

Keisei Skyliner

~100 min total  ·  ~¥2,800  ·  2 transfers

Cheaper, still fast, one more transfer at Nippori. Fine if you like puzzles.

Option A — Narita Express (Recommended)

  1. 1
    Get a Suica IC Card — do this first

    After clearing customs, before anything else: find the green JR ticket machines or the Suica counter. Buy a Suica IC card. It's ¥2,000 total (¥500 refundable deposit + ¥1,500 balance). This card taps you through every train, subway, and bus gate in Tokyo — and also pays for convenience store food, which you will be eating a lot of. Add more credit at any station machine. Do not lose it.

    Terminal 1: B1F near JR East Travel Service Center  ·  Terminal 2: B1F same
  2. 2
    Buy your N'EX ticket

    At the same JR counter or green machines, buy a Narita Express (N'EX) reserved seat to Shinjuku. Cost is approximately ¥3,070. The machines have English. Show the staff "Shinjuku" if needed — they're used to it. The N'EX runs roughly every 30–60 minutes and takes about 80 minutes to Shinjuku.

    Departure boards show "N'EX Narita Express" — look for your platform
  3. 3
    Board the N'EX to Shinjuku

    Find your car and seat number on your ticket. Luggage racks are at the ends of each car. The train is quiet — probably quieter than you're used to. Don't make phone calls. Don't talk loudly. Just sit, look out the window, and accept that you are now in Japan. The ride is about 80 minutes.

  4. 4
    Get off at Shinjuku Station

    Shinjuku is the last stop. You'll exit the N'EX into Shinjuku Station, which is the largest and most labyrinthine train station on earth. Do not panic. You are looking for the Toei Shinjuku Line. Follow signs that say "Toei" and look for the orange/magenta Toei colors. The entrance is within the station complex — tap your Suica at the gate.

    Shinjuku has 200+ exits. You want the Toei Shinjuku Line, not any JR line
  5. 5
    Board the Toei Shinjuku Line toward Motoyawata (本八幡)

    Platform is inside the Toei section. Board any train heading toward Motoyawata (本八幡) or Hashimoto (橋本) — both pass through Kikukawa. The trains come every few minutes. Count 11 stops: Shinjuku-Sanchome → Akebonobashi → Ichigaya → Kudanshita → Jimbocho → Ogawamachi → Iwamotocho → Bakuroyokoyama → Hamacho → Morishita → Kikukawa. Station names are displayed in English inside the train.

    Cost from Shinjuku to Kikukawa: ~¥280 via Suica
  6. 6
    Exit at Kikukawa, take Exit A2

    Get off at Kikukawa Station (菊川). Take Exit A2. You're now in your neighborhood. Comfy Home Kikukawa is a short walk from the exit. If confused, open Google Maps — it works perfectly in Tokyo and will walk you in via satellite if needed.

Option B — Keisei Skyliner (Budget)

  1. 1
    Get a Suica IC Card (same as above)

    Do this first regardless of which route you take.

  2. 2
    Find the Keisei Line and board the Skyliner to Nippori

    Look for signs to the Keisei line — it's a separate ticketing system from JR. Buy a Keisei Skyliner ticket to Nippori (~¥2,570). The Skyliner is a separate express train (not the N'EX) and takes about 40 minutes to Nippori. It's fast and comfortable, just a bit less obvious to navigate at the airport.

  3. 3
    At Nippori, transfer to JR Yamanote Line toward Shinjuku

    From Nippori Station, follow signs to the JR Yamanote Line (the green circular line on all maps). Board heading toward Shinjuku (inner loop, about 5 stops through Ueno, Akihabara, etc.). About 25 minutes. Tap Suica at the gate.

  4. 4
    From Shinjuku, continue as per Option A (Steps 4–6)

    Same from here: Toei Shinjuku Line, 11 stops to Kikukawa, Exit A2.

Your Backyard

The Neighborhood

Kikukawa sits between two underrated Tokyo corridors — the old Sumida riverside to the east, and the increasingly hip Kiyosumi-Shirakawa coffee district to the south. Ryogoku (the sumo heartland) is a short walk north. You lucked out.

~15 min walk north

Ryogoku

The neighborhood that feeds and houses sumo wrestlers. The Kokugikan arena is here — even outside tournament seasons it's worth the walk. The chanko nabe (wrestler's stew, essentially a large hot pot) restaurants in the area are genuinely excellent and not particularly expensive. You'll eat a lot.

~10 min walk south

Kiyosumi-Shirakawa

Tokyo's serious coffee neighborhood. Third-wave cafes, design studios, a beautiful garden from the Meiji era. Quieter than most Tokyo neighborhoods, which is either great or suspicious depending on your personality. The coffee is not a joke — this is where Japanese barista culture is very serious about itself, and you should let it be.

~20 min by transit

Monzen-Nakacho

A historic merchant district with a shrine at its center and an izakaya street that gets progressively louder as the night goes on. Great place for your first proper Japanese bar experience — just order by pointing and say "hitotsu" (one) when you want something. Works every time.

~5 min walk

Sumida Riverside

Walk east and you'll hit the Sumida River. The riverside paths are great for evening walks — nice views of the Skytree tower lit up against the sky, quiet enough that you can actually think, and a useful reminder that Tokyo has more than just neon and crowds.

Food

Eat & Drink Nearby

You are in one of the best food cities on the planet. Manage expectations upward.

Ryogoku, ~15 min walk

Chanko Nabe

The traditional hot pot served at sumo stables. Large, deeply savory, built for people who need to gain weight. Several restaurants in the Ryogoku area specialize in it — look for ones advertising "chanko" (ちゃんこ). Kirishima and Tomozuna are well-regarded names. Order the set course and eat too much. This is correct behavior.

Kiyosumi-Shirakawa, ~10 min walk

Third-Wave Coffee

The Kiyosumi-Shirakawa area is where Tokyo's serious coffee culture lives. Omotesando Coffee and Fuglen Tokyo (originally from Oslo, which is fine) have outposts nearby. The coffee is excellent and the rooms are usually very quiet, which may feel unsettling if you're used to Starbucks. Lean into it.

Morishita area, walkable

Local Izakayas

The streets between Kikukawa and Morishita have small neighborhood izakayas (Japanese gastropubs) that get lively after 7pm. No English menu, no tourist pricing, no problem — just point at what people around you are eating and order a beer first. The staff will figure it out. This is consistently the best way to eat in Tokyo.

Ideas

Itinerary Ideas

Two optional side trips you mentioned. Both are good half-day adventures.

Kasukabe, Saitama · day trip

Underground Flood Prevention Tunnel (G-Cans)

Official name: Metropolitan Area Outer Underground Discharge Channel

The giant underground flood-control chamber with concrete columns ("underground temple" vibe). Tours are limited and require advance booking, so this is a plan-ahead stop.

Transit idea: Kikukawa -> Shinjuku Line to Jimbocho -> Hanzomon Line to Kitasenju -> Tobu line toward Kasukabe area, then local bus/taxi to the site.

Machida · west Tokyo edge

Machida Retro Mall Stop

Plan: check out the older, worn-in mall vibe around Machida Station

Machida has a mix of big commercial blocks and older shopping interiors that feel more lived-in than central Tokyo malls. Good for photos, random finds, and a slower half-day wandering plan.

Transit idea: Kikukawa -> Shinjuku Line to Shinjuku -> Odakyu Odawara Line rapid express to Machida.

Transit

Getting Around

Tokyo's train system is not as complicated as it looks. Use Google Maps for navigation — it knows every line, every transfer, every exit. Tap Suica on the way in, tap Suica on the way out. That's it.

~20 min  ·  Direct

Shinjuku

The most chaotic square kilometer in Japan. Neon, crowds, infinite food, Kabukicho at night (the famous red-light district — worth seeing even just as spectacle), and the world's busiest train station. Useful as a transit hub and overwhelming as a destination. Both are correct.

  • →
  • Toei Shinjuku Line (toward Motoyawata)
  • →
  • Shinjuku (11 stops)
~30 min  ·  1 Transfer

Shibuya

The famous scramble crossing, hundreds of teenagers, brand shops stacked ten stories high, and the Hachiko statue (a dog, beloved, long story). Go at night for the full effect of the crossing. Crowded in a way that feels performative, which is sort of the point.

  • Kikukawa
  • →
  • Toei Shinjuku Line → Shinjuku
  • →
  • JR Yamanote Line (2 stops south) → Shibuya
~25 min  ·  1 Transfer

Asakusa

Senso-ji is the oldest temple in Tokyo and the one that looks like every photo you've seen of Japan. Still worth visiting, especially early morning. The surrounding streets have good street food and overpriced but fun souvenirs. The Nakamise market leading to the temple gate has been selling snacks for 300 years — that kind of consistency is worth respecting.

  • Kikukawa
  • →
  • Toei Shinjuku Line → Ogawamachi
  • →
  • Tokyo Metro Ginza Line → Asakusa
~25 min  ·  1 Transfer

Akihabara

Electric Town — stacked electronics shops, retro game floors, anime stores, and a very specific Tokyo energy. Even if you are not shopping, it is worth a walk for the atmosphere and side-street ramen.

  • Kikukawa
  • →
  • Toei Shinjuku Line → Ogawamachi
  • →
  • Short walk to Akihabara Station (JR)
~30 min  ·  1 Transfer

Harajuku & Meiji Shrine

Meiji Shrine is a large forested Shinto shrine in the middle of the city — genuinely peaceful in a way that makes you forget you're surrounded by 14 million people. Harajuku is right next door and is the opposite of peaceful. The famous Takeshita Street is teen fashion at full saturation. Both experiences in one stop. Efficient.

  • Kikukawa
  • →
  • Toei Shinjuku Line → Shinjuku
  • →
  • JR Yamanote Line (3 stops south) → Harajuku
